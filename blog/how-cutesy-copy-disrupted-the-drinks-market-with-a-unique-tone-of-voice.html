How a cutesy tone of voice disrupted the drinks market

Innocent Drinks tone of voice changed both the landscape for consumers and copywriters alike.

By Jade Zienkiewicz
on 8/28/11

I discovered Innocent smoothies as a student in my early 20s. On my lunch break from my temp job, I remember holding an Innocent smoothie in my hand while laughing at the frankly surreal copy on the label.

The ingredients list read: “Approximately half a banana and 3 and a 1/2 grapes”. The tone felt cheerful and a bit cheeky. I loved that they suggested calling them on “the banana phone” (a generational in-joke for anyone who is not an 80s child).

An impression was made. I was doing a writing and media degree at the time, and informality captivated me anyway. That year there may, or may not have been, an enthusiastic case study of Terry Deary’s Horrible Histories series in my publishing class.

Of course, after this discovery, I wanted to work for this fun drinks business. But I quickly gave up those lofty pretensions to become a life long customer instead.

Copy with zero pretension

innocent drinks logo with a small 'i'. It has a playful tone of voice. What was attractive about their tone of voice was its conversational but straightforward feeling. It was a far cry from the pretentious copy you’d find on ‘health food’ packaging at the time. It also read like it knew it was breaking conventions. Like a young person speaking out of turn in a room full of stuffy old people. This appealed to me, and others it seems. What is marketing if it doesn’t know how to speak to its audience in their language?

Even down to the branding choice of using a lowercase ‘i’ in the name. Journalist Eva Wiseman – an Innocent critic, and very much not a fan – points out that this is to appear as though they are not aligned with big corporate culture. Which is ironic now, because since 2009 they’ve been owned by CocaCola. But she’s correct about that branding decision at the time, and there was something about this that spoke to the wider context of what was happening in the food industry.

It was the right time for a new product, and a fresh tone of voice

Innocent made an impression because of the way the market was at the time. Different food scandals have dominated each decade of the 20th and 21st centuries, and to be fair, they still do. Some may even put Innocent on the list of things to be wary of (fruit doesn’t mean low sugar after all).

But at that time, we’d been through Ribena in the 80s, and then Sunny D in the 90s. The public had been naïve and were paying more attention to the ingredients list on their foods. Too many brands had given the impression that their products were healthy. Innocent seemed like a breath of fresh air. Transparent. And its copy choices affirmed that.

They were market disruptors. A great product, and a strong point of view. They grew quickly.

What followed was an explosion of smoothies and yoghurt drinks from competitors offering similar products at competitive prices. Everyone wanted to capitalise on this ‘new’ idea. Drinks started innovating to emphasise the purity of their ingredients. And whilst Innocent have never claimed to be organic, they are vocal about avoiding suppliers who use the most potent pesticides.

All the elements aligned and the industry was forever altered

Innocent’s growth in the early noughties coincided with the wider changing landscape. In 2004, Super Size Me was released. The documentary was an extreme take on the fast food diet, but it still raised important questions about the role that fast food corporations play in rising obesity rates. It asked whether they are as responsible and accountable as they could be. For the first time, being ‘anti-McDonalds’ was no longer the realm of a May day protester. It’s no wonder brands like Innocent grew.

Innocent was thriving in a time of scepticism to mass produced, low quality foods. Underpinning it all, they had employed a tone of voice and brand personality so potent that it had reverberating effects on the market at large for a couple of decades, beyond even the drinks industry.

It simply changed the field of advertising copy. Award winning copywriter Paul Burke, originator of the brand voice, said he lived to regret this. You can read his “confession” in The Spectator, but in summary, he came up with the concept while working at an ad agency alongside one of Innocent’s founders. When the concept was finished, he was proud of what he’d done, a straightforward tone of voice for a nice honest product. Innocent launched and the brand was taken in-house to live on under their own copywriting team.

Frankenstein is born

Cookies with a playful tone of voice - eat me is written on them in icing. Over time, Burke saw his idea grow beyond his intentions: “…Pride turned to horror as Innocent ‘developed’ my innovation…” he said. “I remember physically recoiling when, on the base of one Innocent carton, it said ‘Stop looking at my bottom’. No, no, no! It wasn’t supposed to be like this.”

A similar message now sits at the footer if their website, and I have to say, it elicited a laugh from me.

“British food packaging now has a matey, at worst babyish, tone …” Sophie Grimshaw lamented in her 2014 article in The Guardian. “My basket of groceries now addresses me as though we are killing time on Facebook. I began to wonder when we arrived at this cringey consensus, and why.”

‘Wackaging’ (wacky packaging) has been in full throttle since Innocent came on the scene. It seemed every new company was asking for ‘an Innocent’ treatment from their brand consultants. New, but similar, tones of voice were being created to charm buyers in the same way. It has lasted for two decades.

Its tentacles even seemingly reached broadcasting. Paul Burke noted: “When I went on The Moral Maze on Radio 4, and professional trendy vicar Giles Fraser addressed me as ‘mate’, I thought I’ve only got myself to blame.” It’s disheartening to hear this from the creator of a clever concept. Sadly, it’s not unusual when creatives relinquish their work to live on in the hands of a corporation.

The question is, is it that bad?

Nick Asbury explores the wackaging phenomena in more detail in his blog here. His commenters make some valid points, like, is it Innocent’s fault that they were so widely copied? It’s just a victim of its own success. It’s hard for me to turn on Innocent for that.

My thoughts on Paul Burke’s concept haven’t changed. It was an original approach. I even think it was inevitable. On speaking to The Guardian, Innocent’s in-house copywriter Lucie Bright said they were always just using the language they use to talk to their friends, and that the cultural shift can only be a good thing. I can’t help but agree. Especially considering my first interaction with the brand. Our culture was changing, becoming less formal. That social shift was always going to be reflected in how businesses communicate to their buyers. The fact that a throng of companies followed Innocent to the point of banality is a testament to its infectious originality.

To me, there’s a contrast between how the general public respond to brands using this tone of voice, and how industry insiders feel about it. For as much as the brand’s voice may have evolved slightly from Burke’s original concept, the informality worked.

“Who wants to be friends with a banana smoothie?” was Eva Wiseman’s question. But why anything in marketing? We could agree that this brand voice raises questions of a culture that infantilizes the consumer. It’s a worthy criticism. But Innocent didn’t actually do that, some its copycats did. And the style seems to have reflected the public’s tastes.

No brand ever made me laugh like Innocent did. I still read the bottles and cartons to see what’s new. The fact that critics pinpoint Innocent as the main culprit for wackaging really shows how prolific they’ve been. People are still talking about it after more than 20 years.

Isn’t that good marketing?
