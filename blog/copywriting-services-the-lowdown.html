Copywriting services: the lowdown

Engaging the right copywriting service is key to ensuring you are building the right relationship with new and existing markets.

By Jade Zienkiewicz
on 8/28/11

Forward-thinking businesses know that high-quality content builds trust and authority, and is key to both long-term customer retention and new business. Yet at times, imaginative content can seen like the last priority of a business, let alone something to be used in strategy.

In this article you will find out how useful engaging with a copywriting service can be. Read on to find out how good writing, how using a professional writer could help bolster your very vital and neglected content strategy.

Any company serious about customer retention and growth should be looking at content as a part of their business strategy. But there are two major problems facing businesses on this front:

Many companies know that they need good content to drive business, but they don’t have the time. This means it can become an afterthought. Decision makers can be guilty of believing that content is the ‘fluffy’ side of corporate activity. This is when there’s a disconnect in the relationship between marketing and what it has to do with the core business. So, in their view, it’s a big investment for little gain. There are many different types of writing in business, but to anyone new to this, there are fundamentally two types of writing activities in business (there are more, but these are the basics):

Copywriting

Copywriting is sales driven. It’s about creating an enticing offering to get someone to click, buy, or sign up. This is the copy on your sales funnels, landing pages, product pages – anything related to getting the user to take an action.

Content writing

Content writing is anything that builds a relationship with your client. Informative, relevant and engaging content that either keeps your existing audience engaged, or builds relationships with new markets in some ways.

These ‘types’ can overlap. For example, packaging copy both sells the product, but gives the brand experience like content would.

All types of copy are going to seamlessly feel as though the reader is engaging with the same ‘personality’, whether they hit your website, social media, or are buying your product in the shop.

It’s just about understanding the aim each time that will mean you are using a different discipline in the field.

That’s why I always say: if you think content is overrated, you’re doing it wrong. What many business owners struggle with is the fact that traditional one-way marketing methods are only one part of a bigger picture. They prioritise sales (and copy activities) over anything else. But developing businesses are increasingly recognising the role of content in their business strategy. They know that to remain competitive, a big business needs to actively engage in relationship building activities.

It’s the long game to customer retention.

It seems obvious, but we get so busy with running the company, we don’t have time for the finer details. But we have to find a way, because these have become major avenues of development for our competitors. Everybody consults the internet to solve their problems, and that’s why written content has never been more important. It should be a priority, because your content is a major component of your brand, and it impacts how customers feel about you.

Copywriting in a sales respect isn’t just about meeting new customers. Many of the best brands know that copy keeps engaged customers loyal. So even if growth isn’t the biggest concern right now, you still need engaging copy to keep your existing clients on board.

Simply put, businesses often don’t want to invest in copywriting services, but it’s the default medium you’re using all the time to communicate to both new and existing audiences.

A copywriting service will work for your business in the following ways

It’s the voice of your brand. This is true for customers and your staff. Your written internal communications can massively affect how your staff view you as a brand (they are ‘buyers’ too!). Copy is about persuasion – it’s how people decide to buy from you: so it literally affects revenue. Copy shows clients how you represent value. Not just the what they are getting, but what your product is worth, which in the best cases, surpasses cost. Your copy helps them create emotional value. It convinces customers of your values (not money, but what you stand for!). Why *you* are worth investing in. This has the potential to create ‘meaningful’ connections, which can help them decide to choose you over a similar product from your competition. Attracts people to engage with you online – think social media (which helps you reach more people). Encourages people to talk about you (this is great for reputation building, and referrals are one of the best ways of getting new custom). That’s why businesses who don’t have an inhouse content writer need to train one, or hire one.

Smart content is a trust building exercise Before we get into why we should even bother with content, let’s cover a very basic thing in business. When have you ever bought from a website that is full of terrible grammar and spelling mistakes? Another question: If you casually hit a website and can’t see straight away exactly what they do, do you go digging for it, or do you close the tab and carry on scrolling through Twitter? And, last, how many times have you seen a badly written post on social media and thought it was a spam account?

If copy is being created anyway, isn’t it better to plan and align it with company values, strategy and mission statements? So we already know from the answers that well-written copy will qualify your leads. This is where a client goes to check you out to do further research and decides if you are trustworthy and worth their investment. You have about 5 seconds to engage a user’s interest. Poorly written content will see customers bounce without thinking twice – they don’t owe you anything. The least you can do is take time to capture their attention.

That’s why great copy is also about great user experience – you are helping to navigate your users to what they need quickly. It’s not just about what you want to say. It’s about learning to say the things you want to communicate while catering to they need. This is copy on a very basic level. Business owners need to put themselves in their customers’ shoes, and understand this before a content strategy is possible. That’s where professional copywriting services add value in the perception of a business.

SEO copywriting services: build an evergreen index that Google thinks is useful

Search engine optimisation (SEO) is not new, but it’s a lot more sophisticated than it used to be. Optimising your website is about increasing your chances of getting a high search ranking in Google and other search engines, and therefore increasing your discoverability without having to pay for traffic to your website via other advertising tools.

Where it gets tricky is that a lot of businesses don’t understand how to implement SEO. It’s not just about having 1,000 word articles with enough repetition of the key word or phrase you are trying to rank for.

SEO is more than that now thanks to Google. The powerhouse search engine continues to develop sophisticated tools that measure the quality of your site. Therefore, SEO now has a more complex sub-set of conditions to be considered worthy in the search ranking. We have to bear in mind that Google’s mission is to index and serve the best quality content in relation to the search the user has given.

Now, can we put a fine point on ‘quality’, please? Because I have hit websites of businesses in the middle of a content strategy, and sadly they’re just producing masses of content with the right ingredients, but it’s not really doing anything. This is not it. It’s got to be stuff people need and want to read about. Google measures how long people spend on your site, and a quick bounce means you haven’t served up the answer they need to their question.

So you need to write meaningful copy, not just things to get visitors to your site. You have content a human actually wants to read 9as opposed to what you think an AI will serve up). In the end the reader decides what’s good, and Google is watching.

The case for a content strategy: why bother writing articles?

Well written content is an investment. Most businesses who engage with proper copywriting services understand that content creation isn’t an empty process of publishing and moving on. It lives on. It’s something that should form part of your business plan, and be created alongside it.

If you come up with a smart strategy, you will be producing material that continues to give value over time, will always be needed, read, and continues to do its job long past its publish date.

Knowledge sharing is a form of relationship building. If you’ve got the answers to someone’s problem, you’re going to make friends.

Remember, when someone is researching a product or service, a lot of work goes in first. They don’t want to speak directly with a business until they’re almost ready to make a purchase. They’ll have already educated themselves adequately by the time they decide to contact you.

So you ned to think about how you will capture your client in th research stage of their buying decision. And this is why content is so vital – it lays the foundations down early and gives you the potential to catch the buyer early.

Content is naturally part of your marketing strategy – you can’t be too busy or strapped for resources – otherwise, you’re creating a stream of copy without an intention at the heart of it.

Intentional copy: everything you create should have a purpose

Content for social media, blogs or awareness reports – all content that should be created with a purpose in mind. So many of us write what we are interested in or what we think our industry should do, without any backing or research to suggest otherwise. That means copy is constantly being created on their digital media without any thought behind it.

When copy is viewed as a by-product of other activities, it’s very rarely created with the company’s real aims in mind. So we’re missing out on the opportunity to let the content work well for the company.

If copy is being created anyway, isn’t it better to plan and align it with company values, strategy and mission statements? At its best, you risk creating bland copy. At its worst, this approach risks preventing you from meeting your audiences, and wasting the money pumped into the products you are creating.

Example: Put it this way – someone hears about you and they take to your social media to find out who you are. They see that you put out a report, one that you invested in, or which is vital to your client-base, and you just tweeted this:

“We were proud to fund this report into climate change, read it to find out what our policy-makers plan to do over the next decade.”

While this tells your audience what it is and what your involvement has been, you haven’t commented on it, analysed it, supported it adequately or even told the reader what is interesting about it.

Is that going to make them want to invest in you?

Say something, stand for something

Example 2: You want to give perspective, opinion, a call to action. You want to make people care and understand why this is important. Instead, we should be aiming for something like:

“We’re asking you to join us in lobbying the Government on their climate change policy. Read our independent research criticising current policy-makers’ inaction and the efficient alternatives we need to invest in over the next decade.”

The supporting content of your media – the copy – will make a difference to how any piece of media is interacted with, and by who. It should be supported by the overarching aims of your brand. Where does this fit into who you are and what problems you solve? What do you want the viewer to do with the information? This filters down from your mission statement, your values, and your aims. No piece of media sits in silo from another, and none of your messaging should either.

“Captions aren’t tacked on afterthoughts” – they add weight and context to their accompanying media.

Copywriting is a major part of your marketing framework

Visual media is on the rise – isn’t that where your focus should be?

Video is proven to be a highly engaging tool that continues to grow, attracting fans and interaction that can win followers and customers. But who is writing the scripts to your video? The social media headlines and by-lines that contextualises them, calls to action, who is creating quality subtitles? Good quality copy is a tool that pulls in viewers. Using it well is a skill that can be learned, but it takes time.

No media exists in a bubble alone, each has different functions, and thought-provoking copy is a vital part of that marketing mix that feeds into the overall content strategy. Let’s not pretend websites are just words, but let’s not pretend they’re only graphics either. You need good graphic design, UX, photography, videos – and much more. Each discipline depends on the other.

When businesses ignore the need for purposeful content, they are missing out on a vital trust building exercise that sets them apart from the competition.

Quality copy, at its most basic function, is the framework that contextualises all of your client-facing activities.

Is your content persuasive?

Even if you have fairly well written client-facing channels that can be easily understood, ask yourself: are you actually persuading your audience? Can you do better?

A professional copywriting services will help you to discover how to differentiate yourself if you’re doing something similar to another company. They will help you figure out the deciding factors to guide a customer to buy with you over a competitor.

It could be the first few lines they are hit with in a Google search – the thing that pulls in the initial hit to your website. It could be a Twitter post about your values. A product description that taps into the customer’s ideas about themselves.

Copywriting and identity

Professional copywriting services don’t just help you communicate an offer – your exchange of goods or services – they help you reflect the identity of the people you want to do business with. They empathise. They tap into people’s self-image and aspirations. Copy helps you show that you understand their problems – and how they can be solved. On top of this, the right words set the tone and energy, giving your words a personality. It shows you know your audience. That is what creates an emotional connection with them and makes a brand memorable. People buy for two reasons: Buying is an emotional process, often based in reflection of values. People want to out their money into worthy things. Good copy communicates what your business stands for, helping them to make the right decision. A copywriters’ job is to ensure every level of copy is persuading the customer, that you are worth their emotional and financial investment.

It’s the difference between a quality and low grade offering, cheap or expensive. And even the cheapest products will still invest time into copy, because pitching to the right audience is everything if you want to sell.

Words communicate the right ideas about your brand to reach the right buyers. People buy things that they think speak to them. Copy is one of the many factors that helps them make a connection to your offering. It’s the copywriting service provider’s job to convince browsers that they will feel part of something in buying your product, whatever that may be.

Free content is not a distraction from selling

David Meermen-Scott, Author and marketing entrepreneur, is an excellent example of how his free content keeps giving. He’s sold over a million books in 29 languages on marketing.

In particular, his book The News Rules of Marketing and PR has sold 400,000 copies. And all because of his blog that he started in 2004. This blog was eventually published as a book, and is now in its 6th edition. This blog, and free e-books, continue to drive hits to his site, even blogs that are over 10 years old.

Yet he still sells books. Despite all this free content. Because people want more of his expertise, despite all the free stuff. His content is rich and people still pay for it.

He proves that people who benefit from your content are likely to become customers later on.

Copy has to be useful: The ‘what’s in it for me?’ concept

People often mistake copywriting and content creation as a way to make a company look good. It is that but it’s also so much more. Quality content should be benefit-oriented, otherwise nobody will read it. You’ve got to genuinely seek to solve a problem. This might not lead to a direct sale on a first read, but it should build the foundations for a relationship in the future. Good content encourages people to come back to you for answers in the future. And done right, it earns respect.

And it’s not just about blogging, writing newsletters, or even creating a video. We are talking about everything visible to your buying audience. It encompasses the entirety of your communications, starting from your website copy, right along with what you are writing on your social media . It’s meant to be making your customers fans, entertaining them, so they’ll not just buy your products but will enjoy their interactions with you, and with some luck, will prompt them to talk about you positively to their friends.

Any company doing well in their social channels will know that sometimes, their audience just simply wants to be entertained. When people want a break and go to Facebook, if they see something funny they will interact with you and share your material – and become curious about your offering.

This takes time, but it is a way of reaching your potential customers, increasing your discoverability and showcasing your expertise. The best companies are already doing this, and they use professional copywriting services, or they have in-house talent dedicated to this.

Thought leadership

Content impacts your authority with competitors and customers

Many companies are now generating their own research into the issues facing their respective industries, and one of the related aspects of this is awareness reports – or a white paper. The ultimate in thought leadership.

These are often used in the technology space to create awareness around new approaches to known problems. But many industries are now recognising the use of them. They can help to create a positive contribution to your reputation and to set you apart from your competitors in the marketplace of ideas.

It’s not an advert

Whilst an awareness report is an advert of sorts, it’s actually not about you or your product. It’s about your buyer, and their problems. It’s about how you can help solve those problems with your expert knowledge.

Awareness reports simply define problems and offer solutions.

But why should you give away your knowledge for free? Surely you should be monetising this?

Because, done well, they can prompt action from buyers. Good content can retain loyalty and convert a reader into a buyer later on. It can prompt them to share your material, or talk about you, and get your brand out there to a wider audience.

Awareness reports are not the be all and end all of content marketing. They should form a small part of a wider content strategy which covers all your public facing channels as outlined above.

“We don’t have the resources…”

You don’t have to commission expensive research to create good content. In fact, many companies take for granted all your in-house knowledge and forget that your average client could use a bit of education – after all, aren’t they trying to understand what it is that you know?

The best content answers problems and showcases your company knowledge

There are experts in your company that can and should be utilised to create useful content.

To transform their knowledge into useful content, you can go three routes:

Get a professional copywriting service on board to interview your experts: The Society of Authors have a directory that will be able to help you find someone suitable for your field. It’s time-efficient for the person who hasn’t got time to do the writing. This is a really good idea if your in-house experts aren’t natural writers. It won’t be as expensive as you think, and it’s worth the money. Get your expert to do the writing: and, if necessary, you can outsource the editing to a copywriter/editor tidy the copy up and knock it into shape. Ideal if you don’t have a big budget but your expert needs some steer. Long term: Look internally to see if you have existing talent that you can hone. Getting into content creation means you may want to consider making this someone’s job. If you’re going the DIY route, two things are a must: 1) the person writing has to have a proven interest and talent in writing, and 2) they should be rewarded – writing is work too. Remember, that if this activity has been neglected so far in your strategy, then it needs to be part of somebody’s job legitimately, and recognised. If your writer doesn’t have a marketing background, then a certain amount of direction and planning from management and the experts on the aim of the piece will be required beforehand.

Get your own copywriter: Training in-house talent

An alternative to employing professional copywriting services is to train in house talent. This is often seen as expensive, but the benefit of this is that you get to train your own specialist in your field. There are lots of training courses that your appointed internal budding writer can do. Find something to help guide them over at the Chartered Institute of Marketing or the London School of Journalism to get them started. Investing in someone in the company with natural talent and having them formally trained could win loyalty to the company – they will get a better in-depth feel for the business and will soon be able to produce content independently.

A word of advice though. Do not give this job to someone who doesn’t really want it. This is for someone who sees themselves as growing with you in this field. If they don’t have an interest in writing, show zest for the company, or their existing stream of work means they couldn’t possibly fit it in, then you need to rethink who should do it.

Remember, the quality of the content must be powerful – if you’re putting resources aside for this, make it count.

Why would I give away my secrets?

Audience education has a high conversion rate. You may be worried that your competitors are lifting ideas, but in the long run, building trust with your audience is what gets you customers. Because often they’ll choose who they feel they have a meaningful connection with. Audiences are very adept at knowing who is genuine. So don’t worry about your competitors.

Helpful content puts you into the sales consideration bracket of the buyer thought process. If you prove your authority in an area effectively, you become trusted within your market.

For example, I recently used a recipe from Cupcakes and Doilies owner Gemma Wilson. I was so impressed with the recipe, I visited the shop for lunch when I was in London. This is how content operates in the marketing funnel.

Approach your content authentically

You have to take a genuine approach to your content. If you’re a serious company, with a more traditional culture, you need to be authentic to that, and communicate with your clients in a tone they will understand and expect.

But remember, there’s no hard sale in content. The market is intelligent to sham ‘infomercial’ content. This isn’t an exercise in “I’m giving you this, now buy my product”. Your reader owes you nothing. Prove you’re worth their time, contribute to the conversation for the greater good, something that is greater than your product. And they will come back to you anyway.

Content is about knowledge, branding, and thought leadership first, and sales later. Think of it as going to a networking event. You don’t say your name and state what you’re selling. You go to make friends in your industry first. That’s what the content game is.

Good quality content + readers = potential buyers.

There will be lots of consumers reading your stuff that don’t become customers – that’s fine. You’re educating your market, getting your brand out there. You’re a good guy in your industry, gaining authority and respect in your field. It’s likely your peers in your industry will be emulating you too, which is a good position to be in.

Content is as much about reputation as it is anything else. And that’s something you can’t buy.

Cornering your content

If you want to start publishing content, then you need to start thinking like a publisher – your copywriting service will be able to guide you, but if you’re going it alone, ask yourself what your typical customer looks like. There will be a number of ‘types’. What problems do they face? There will be more than one. What knowledge do you hold that is going to help them with their problems? That’s the content you’re going to write about.

Where to start

Consult your internal experts. What’s their knowledge, what do they know on a deep level that your customers are looking for? Look at common incoming queries: Consult your customer service teams – what are your customers top ten problems that they ask you to solve? Can you create ‘how to’ guides based on this information? Do you have expert legal knowledge that holds value that is going to help potential clients? Arm yourself with information about your buyers. What are they looking for on Google? What are they searching for on your site? Are there any gaps in the content market that you can fill that will be helpful? It may only be connected to your service, but publishing content that helps your buyer means you’re going to capture their attention. If you share your expertise on a subject they need, they’re going to remember you for it. You’ll win loyalty, and fans.

Want to understand more on cornering copywriting and content for a niche? See our piece on how the coaching industry is using copy and content to niche toward new audiences on the basis of shared values.

Put yourself in their shoes For instance, you are a property developer, and you happen to know a lot about the challenges facing landlords. Your company doesn’t directly carry out services for landlords, but is still within your field of expertise. However, you produce content advising landlords on whatever issues they face, and how they can overcome them – structural issues, damp, attracting the right tenants, health and safety, potential legal pitfalls and how to protect themselves and their tenants.

If they find your content useful and entertaining, they’ll remember you. You potentially have a future business partner or client in property development.

And when someone talks to their friends about an idea they had, or something they need, a reader of your content may well suggest this great business they know of because they subscribe to their material. A recommendation has so much value – people trust their friends.

Putting yourself in your customer’s shoes will help you to form a more holistic content strategy. The blogs you should write, how to improve your landing pages and website copy, how to use your social media channels better. It will show you where to rewrite and improve your messaging, and understand what other content you could be producing.

Create content that efficiently drives action. Present the problem, show them your answer to their problem, and then finally tell them how to take action – funnel them. Make sure they know what to do once you’ve educated them.

Focus on being helpful. Then when your client needs your product, they’ll remember you.

In short: make it useful and enjoyable to read. Break down difficult information so it’s easy to digest. And make it easy to find.

Ready for the next steps

Review copy on a basic level: both client and staff facing, if possible. Strategise: Outline what is and isn’t working, and draw up a plan of action. Drum up support: If you need support from decision makers, now is the time to lobby them and show them where you could do better, and how the best are already doing it. Be good for the community: get ready to share a good portion of your knowledge (without giving away all the goods). Create relationships: your content should make the right emotional connections to your brand. Think like a publisher: corner your content by creating information needed by your prospective clients. What sort of specialised content you could be making? Take it seriously: Remember that content is an investment. Make it somebody’s job and reward them, or outsource to a professional. Have a budget: try not to skimp on this important reputational process. Engage invested staff and get them on board. Be a thought leader in your field.
